### **1.1 WHAT IS POSITIONING? (Simple Definition)**

**Positioning is:** How you define your product's place in customers' minds relative to alternatives.

**Not marketing.** Not advertising. Not taglines.

**It's strategy:** The filter through which ALL marketing/sales decisions flow.

**April Dunford's Definition:**

"Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about."

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ THE COST OF POOR POSITIONING │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

BAD POSITIONING \= DEATH BY A THOUSAND CUTS

Sales Issues: ├─ Long sales cycles (6+ months → 18+ months) ├─ Low win rates (\<20% vs 40%+ with good positioning) ├─ Discounting pressure (can't justify premium price) ├─ Wrong customers (churn in 3-6 months) └─ Sales reps confused ("How do I describe this?")

Marketing Issues: ├─ Low conversion rates (\<1% vs 3-5% with clarity) ├─ High CAC (burning cash on wrong audience) ├─ Generic messaging (sounds like everyone else) ├─ Content doesn't resonate (low engagement) └─ Paid ads underperform (no differentiation)

Product Issues: ├─ Feature bloat (trying to be everything to everyone) ├─ Roadmap chaos (no clear "why" for features) ├─ Poor product-market fit (solving wrong problems) └─ Low NPS (\<30 vs \>50 with right positioning)

Business Impact: ├─ Fundraising struggles (investors don't "get it") ├─ Valuation discount (25-50% lower without clarity) ├─ Talent acquisition harder (can't explain vision) └─ Acquisition appeal lower (unclear strategic fit)

GOOD POSITIONING \= FORCE MULTIPLIER

✅ Sales velocity 2-3x faster ✅ Win rates 2x higher ✅ CAC 40-60% lower ✅ Retention 30-50% higher ✅ Pricing power (10-30% premium) ✅ Team alignment (everyone tells same story) ✅ Fundraising easier (clear narrative)

CRITICAL INSIGHT: The order matters\! ───────────────────────────────────── Most companies start with "What category?" (WRONG) Correct order: WHO → WHAT → ALTERNATIVES → DIFFERENCE → VALUE → CATEGORY

Why? Category choice should FOLLOW from who cares about your unique value, not dictate it.

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ OBVIOUSLY AWESOME: THE 5 COMPONENTS │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

1. COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVES ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ "What would customers use if your product didn't exist?" │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    NOT: "Who are your competitors?" (too narrow) YES: "What's the alternative behavior?"

    Examples: ├─ Slack alternative: Email \+ phone calls \+ meetings ├─ Notion alternative: Google Docs \+ Trello \+ Wiki (3 tools) ├─ IRIS alternative: Manual processes \+ spreadsheets \+ SailPoint └─ Superhuman alternative: Gmail (free) \+ manual workflows

    Why this matters: ├─ Defines your comparison set (not just direct competitors) ├─ Helps customers understand "jump" they're making └─ Frames value (what are they escaping FROM?)

    How to find this: ├─ Ask customers: "What were you using before us?" ├─ Ask lost deals: "What did you choose instead?" └─ Sales discovery: "How are you solving this today?"

    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2. UNIQUE ATTRIBUTES (Features) ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ "What can you do that alternatives can't?" │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Must be: ├─ Defensible (not easily copied) ├─ Provable (can demonstrate) └─ Meaningful (customers care)

    NOT unique attributes: ❌ "We have great customer service" (subjective, undefensible) ❌ "AI-powered" (everyone says this) ❌ "Easy to use" (relative, hard to prove)

    GOOD unique attributes: ✅ "300+ pre-built SaaS connectors" (vs 50-80 for competitors) ├─ Defensible: Years to build ├─ Provable: Can list them └─ Meaningful: Customers need SaaS coverage

    ✅ "60-day deployment guarantee" (vs 12-18 months SailPoint) ├─ Defensible: Standardized methodology ├─ Provable: Customer testimonials with dates └─ Meaningful: \#1 buyer fear \= implementation risk

    ✅ "Patented discovery engine with 9 methods" ├─ Defensible: Patent protection ├─ Provable: Patent number └─ Meaningful: Customers need 100% visibility

    How to identify: ├─ Compare feature matrix vs alternatives ├─ Ask engineers: "What's technically hard to replicate?" ├─ Ask customers: "What surprised you about our product?" └─ Win/loss analysis: "Why did they choose us?"

    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3. VALUE (That Attributes Enable) ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ "What business outcomes do unique attributes create?" │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    CRITICAL: Features → Benefits → VALUE

    Example 1: Feature: "300+ SaaS connectors" Benefit: "Govern entire SaaS stack without custom code" VALUE: "$420K saved from license reclamation" \+ "90% faster audit prep"

    Example 2: Feature: "60-day deployment" Benefit: "Live in Q1, not Q3 next year" VALUE: "ROI in 4 months, not 18 months" \+ "No $1M consultant fees"

    Example 3: Feature: "AI-powered risk scoring" Benefit: "Prioritize access reviews by risk" VALUE: "90% time savings on quarterly reviews" \+ "Pass SOX audit first try"

    Mapping Exercise:

    Unique Attribute Benefit Value (Outcome) ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ 9 discovery methods → Find 100% of apps → Eliminate shadow IT risk ($2M avg breach cost avoided)

    iPaaS engine → Build custom connects → No vendor lock-in in 2 weeks (flexibility \= strategic)

    Pre-built templates → Deploy in 60 days → Faster ROI realization (payback in 4mo vs 18mo)

    How to articulate value: ├─ Quantify whenever possible (time, cost, risk) ├─ Use customer language ("We saved 180 hours/quarter") ├─ Map to buyer priorities (IT=productivity, CISO=risk, CFO=cost) └─ Prove it (case studies, before/after metrics)

    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

4. TARGET CUSTOMER SEGMENTS (Who Cares Most) ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ "Who cares MOST about the value we deliver?" │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    NOT: "Everyone could use this" (death sentence) YES: "Mid-market IT teams managing 100+ SaaS apps"

    Segmentation dimensions:

    Firmographic: ├─ Company size: 500-5,000 employees (not \<100, not \>10K) ├─ Industry: B2B SaaS, FinTech, Healthcare (regulated) ├─ Revenue: $10M-$500M ARR ├─ Growth stage: Series B-D (growing fast, need systems) └─ Geography: US, EU (regulatory drivers)

    Technographic: ├─ SaaS adoption: 100+ SaaS apps (heavy users) ├─ Tech stack: Okta/Azure AD \+ cloud infrastructure ├─ Maturity: Adopting SSO, entering compliance phase └─ IT sophistication: 5-20 person IT team

    Behavioral: ├─ Buying behavior: IT-led, 3-6 month sales cycle ├─ Pain intensity: Manual access reviews, audit stress ├─ Alternatives: Using spreadsheets OR outgrowing Okta OR frustrated with SailPoint └─ Trigger events: First SOC2 audit, M\&A, security incident

    Psychographic (Buyer Persona-level): ├─ Goals: Scale IT without scaling headcount ├─ Fears: Audit failure, security breach, career risk ├─ Values: Speed, simplicity, proven solutions └─ Decision style: Research-heavy, reference calls, POC

    Who cares MOST about IRIS value?

    ✅ PERFECT FIT: ├─ Mid-market IT Directors (750-2,500 employees) ├─ Managing 100-300 SaaS apps (sprawl pain) ├─ Entering compliance phase (first SOC2/ISO) ├─ Budget: $150K-$400K for IGA solution ├─ Pain: Manual JML, audit stress, spreadsheet hell └─ Trigger: "We can't keep doing this manually"

    ⚠️ POOR FIT: ├─ Fortune 500 with existing SailPoint (switching costs too high) ├─ Startups \<250 employees (insufficient pain) ├─ Microsoft-first orgs (Entra "free" perceived value) └─ Cost-minimizers (want cheapest, not best)

    The "Who Cares" Test: For each unique attribute, ask: "Which segment would pay 2x more for this capability?"

    If answer is "no one would pay more", it's not differentiating If answer is "everyone would pay more", segment too broad If answer is "mid-market IT teams scaling fast", BINGO

5. MARKET CATEGORY ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ "What market category makes our value obvious?" │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Category \= Context for understanding value

    Three Category Strategies:

    A) EXISTING CATEGORY (Safest) Play in established category, claim differentiation

    Example: IRIS in "Identity Governance (IGA)" ├─ Pro: Buyers already searching for IGA ├─ Pro: Budget exists ("IGA" in IT budget line) ├─ Pro: No market education needed ├─ Con: Crowded (SailPoint, Saviynt, Okta, Microsoft) ├─ Con: Category defined by incumbent (SailPoint) └─ Strategy: Differentiate on speed/simplicity/SaaS-first

    B) SUBCATEGORY (Medium Risk) Create new subcategory within existing category

    Example: "Operational IGA" (vs "Compliance IGA") ├─ Pro: Differentiation through redefinition ├─ Pro: Still has IGA context (buyers understand) ├─ Pro: Attracts specific segment (IT vs GRC buyers) ├─ Con: Some market education required ├─ Con: May confuse if too niche └─ Strategy: "IGA built for IT productivity, not just audits"

    C) NEW CATEGORY (Highest Risk, Highest Reward) Define entirely new category

    Example: Drift's "Conversational Marketing" ├─ Pro: Own 70%+ market share if successful ├─ Pro: Define the rules (what matters, what doesn't) ├─ Pro: Premium pricing (educate \= premium) ├─ Con: 2-3 year market education (expensive) ├─ Con: Requires thought leadership, events, content ├─ Con: Needs clear "old world → new world" narrative └─ Strategy: Only if massive market shift \+ resources

    Category Selection Decision Tree:

    Q1: Do buyers already have budget for a category? ├─ Yes → Consider existing or subcategory └─ No → Category creation required (or change positioning)

    Q2: Does existing category context help or hurt? ├─ Help → Use existing category \+ differentiation └─ Hurt → Subcategory or new category

    Q3: Is your value obvious in existing category? ├─ Yes → Existing category ├─ Maybe → Subcategory └─ No → New category

    Q4: Do you have $5M+ for market education? ├─ Yes → New category viable └─ No → Stick to existing/subcategory

    IRIS Category Decision:

    Option A: "IGA" (existing) ├─ Pro: $4.2B market, established budget ├─ Con: Defined by SailPoint (complexity) └─ Differentiate on: Speed, SaaS-first, IT productivity

    Option B: "Operational IGA" (subcategory) ├─ Pro: Differentiates from compliance-first IGA ├─ Pro: Resonates with IT buyers (42% of budget) └─ Risk: Minimal; still has IGA context

    Option C: "SaaS Access Governance" (new) ├─ Pro: Unique positioning (converge SMP \+ IGA) ├─ Con: Market education burden, unclear budget └─ Risk: High; requires evangelism

    RECOMMENDATION: Option B (Operational IGA) ├─ Leverage existing IGA market ├─ Differentiate from incumbent positioning └─ Attract growing IT-led buyer segment

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ THE 10-STEP POSITIONING WORKSHOP │ │ (Run with cross-functional team: Product, Sales, Exec) │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

PREP WORK (Before Workshop): ├─ Schedule: 4-hour block, no interruptions ├─ Participants: CEO, Product, Sales, Marketing (6-8 people max) ├─ Materials: Whiteboard, sticky notes, Obviously Awesome book └─ Pre-read: Send framework, customer interviews, win/loss data

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STEP 1: CUSTOMER INTERVIEWS (2-4 weeks before) Time: 10-20 interviews, 30-45 min each Who to interview: ├─ Best customers (high NPS, successful, evangelist) ├─ Recent wins (why they chose you) ├─ Recent losses (why they chose competitor) └─ Long-term customers (why they stayed)

Questions to ask:

1. "What were you using before us?"  
2. "What problem were you trying to solve?"  
3. "Why did you start looking for a solution?"  
4. "What alternatives did you evaluate?"  
5. "Why did you choose us?"  
6. "What do you value most about our product?"  
7. "What would you tell a colleague to convince them?"  
8. "If we disappeared tomorrow, what would you do?"  
9. "What category would you put us in?"  
10. "Who else should use our product?"

Document: ├─ Verbatim quotes (especially value descriptions) ├─ Patterns (repeated themes across interviews) └─ Surprises (unexpected uses, values, alternatives)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STEP 2: LIST FEATURES (30 minutes) Brainstorm: What can the product do? ├─ Everyone writes features on sticky notes (1 per note) ├─ No filtering yet; quantity over quality └─ Post on wall, group similar features

Example output: ├─ Access certifications ├─ Role management ├─ 300+ SaaS connectors ├─ 60-day deployment ├─ AI risk scoring ├─ iPaaS engine ├─ etc. (50-100 features)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STEP 3: LIST COMPETITIVE ALTERNATIVES (45 minutes) Question: "What would customers use if we didn't exist?"

Brainstorm all alternatives (not just competitors): ├─ Direct competitors (SailPoint, Okta, Saviynt) ├─ Partial solutions (Okta SSO \+ spreadsheets) ├─ DIY (manual processes, IT helpdesk) ├─ Status quo (do nothing, accept risk) └─ Adjacent categories (SaaS Management Platforms)

Group alternatives by "closeness": ├─ Closest: Direct IGA competitors ├─ Medium: IAM platforms with some IGA ├─ Furthest: Manual processes, spreadsheets └─ Other: SMP tools entering IGA space

Pick 3-5 primary alternatives to focus on: Example:

1. SailPoint (enterprise IGA leader)  
2. Okta IGA (integrated platform)  
3. Manual processes \+ spreadsheets (status quo)  
4. Microsoft Entra (Microsoft-first orgs)

Create comparison matrix:

STEP 9: WRITE POSITIONING STATEMENT (30 minutes) Formula: "For \[target customer\] who \[problem\], \[Product\] is a \[category\] that \[value\]. Unlike \[alternatives\], we \[unique capability\]."

IRIS Example: "For mid-market IT teams managing 100+ SaaS applications who are drowning in manual access management and audit stress, IRIS is an Operational IGA platform that automates 85% of access workflows while delivering audit-ready compliance. Unlike legacy IGA tools that take 18 months to implement or limited platforms that lack enterprise features, IRIS deploys in 60 days and governs your entire SaaS stack with 300+ pre-built connectors."

Breakdown: ├─ Target: "mid-market IT teams managing 100+ SaaS apps" ├─ Problem: "drowning in manual access management and audit stress" ├─ Category: "Operational IGA platform" ├─ Value: "automates 85% of workflows \+ audit-ready compliance" ├─ Alternatives: "legacy IGA (18 months) or limited platforms (lacks features)" └─ Unique: "60 days \+ 300+ SaaS connectors"

One-Sentence Version (Elevator Pitch): "IRIS is Operational IGA for mid-market IT teams—enterprise features with 60-day deployment, not 18-month implementations."

Test Your Positioning Statement: ├─ 5-Second Test: Can someone understand what you do in 5 seconds? YES ├─ Grandma Test: Can non-expert understand? YES (even if they don't know IGA) ├─ Differentiation Test: Is it clear how you're different? YES (60 days vs 18mo) ├─ Value Test: Is business outcome clear? YES (automation \+ compliance) └─ Target Test: Is audience specific? YES (mid-market IT teams)

STEP 10: CREATE VALIDATION PLAN (15 minutes) How to test if positioning resonates:

A) Sales Test (2-4 weeks) ├─ Train sales team on new positioning ├─ A/B test: Old deck vs new deck ├─ Measure: Win rate, sales cycle, deal size └─ Goal: 20%+ improvement in one metric

B) Customer Test (1 week) ├─ Show positioning to 10 best customers ├─ Ask: "Does this describe us? Would you use these words?" ├─ Measure: "Yes, exactly" vs "Hmm, not quite" └─ Goal: 8/10 say "yes, exactly"

C) Prospect Test (2 weeks) ├─ Update homepage with new messaging ├─ Measure: Conversion rate (visitor → demo request) ├─ Goal: 20%+ lift in conversion └─ Alternative: A/B test old vs new hero section

D) Competitive Test (4 weeks) ├─ Use new positioning in competitive deals ├─ Measure: Win rate vs each competitor ├─ Goal: Win rate vs SailPoint improves └─ Track: Objections that disappear

E) Content Test (4 weeks) ├─ Write blog post using new positioning ├─ Measure: Engagement (time on page, shares, comments) ├─ Goal: 2x average engagement └─ Listen: What language do commenters use?

Iteration Triggers: ├─ If sales team doesn't use it → Too complex or doesn't resonate ├─ If customers confused → Category unclear or value not obvious ├─ If prospects bounce → Messaging not compelling └─ If win rate unchanged → Differentiation not meaningful

Commit to Positioning for 6 Months: ├─ Don't change every week ├─ Give it time to compound ├─ Measure consistently └─ Iterate based on data, not opinions

   ❌ Crowded space  
   ❌ Defined by incumbents  
   ❌ Must be significantly better (not 10% better)  
     
   Examples:  
   ├─ Superhuman: "The better email client" (vs Gmail)  
   ├─ Notion: "The better productivity tool" (vs Google Docs)  
   └─ IRIS: "The better IGA" (vs SailPoint)  
     
   Key Messages:  
   ├─ "Everything you expect from \[category\]"  
   ├─ "Without the \[incumbent pain point\]"  
   └─ "At \[fraction\] of the cost/time/complexity"

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2\. THE DISRUPTOR  
   ─────────────  
   Strategy: Redefine category around new dimension  
     
   Template: "The \[new attribute\] \[category\]"  
   Example: "The operational IGA" (vs compliance IGA)  
     
   When to use:  
   ├─ Incumbent defines category around dimension you're weak on  
   ├─ Market shift makes new dimension more important  
   └─ You're stronger on new dimension than incumbents  
     
   Pros:  
   ✅ Differentiation through redefinition  
   ✅ Attracts different buyer persona  
   ✅ Less direct competition  
     
   Cons:  
   ❌ Some market education required  
   ❌ Incumbent can pivot  
   ❌ Risk of being seen as "niche"  
     
   Examples:  
   ├─ Dollar Shave Club: "Affordable razors" (vs Gillette's "best shave")  
   ├─ Tesla: "Software-defined car" (vs "performance/luxury car")  
   └─ IRIS: "Operational IGA" (vs "compliance IGA")  
     
   Key Messages:  
   ├─ "The market has been focused on \[old dimension\]"  
   ├─ "But what really matters is \[new dimension\]"  
   └─ "We're the only \[category\] built for \[new dimension\]"

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3\. THE CATEGORY CREATOR  
   ────────────────────  
   Strategy: Define entirely new category  
     
   Template: "The \[new category\] platform"  
   Example: Drift's "Conversational Marketing"  
     
   When to use:  
   ├─ Existing categories don't describe what you do  
   ├─ Your value is lost when lumped with competitors  
   ├─ You have $5M+ and 2-3 years for evangelism  
   └─ Major market shift enables new approach  
     
   Pros:  
   ✅ Own 70%+ market share if successful  
   ✅ Define the rules (what matters)  
   ✅ Premium pricing (scarcity)  
     
   Cons:  
   ❌ Massive market education cost  
   ❌ No existing budget (must create)  
   ❌ 2-3 year timeline to adoption  
   ❌ High risk of failure  
     
   Examples:  
   ├─ Salesforce: "SaaS" (vs on-premise software)  
   ├─ HubSpot: "Inbound Marketing" (vs outbound)  
   ├─ Drift: "Conversational Marketing" (vs forms/email)  
   └─ Workday: "Cloud ERP" (vs Oracle/SAP)  
     
   Requirements:  
   ├─ Clear "old world → new world" narrative  
   ├─ Thought leadership (books, events, content)  
   ├─ Community building (champions, advocates)  
   ├─ Analyst relations (educate Gartner/Forrester)  
   └─ Patience (3 years to mainstream)  
     
   Key Messages:  
   ├─ "The old way: \[painful status quo\]"  
   ├─ "The change: \[what shifted in world\]"  
   ├─ "The new way: \[your new category\]"  
   └─ "We're leading the \[new category\] movement"

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

4\. THE BRIDGE  
   ──────────  
   Strategy: Combine two existing categories  
     
   Template: "\[Category A\] \+ \[Category B\]"  
   Example: "IGA \+ SaaS Management Platform"  
     
   When to use:  
   ├─ Customers currently using 2+ tools  
   ├─ Integration/data sync between tools is painful  
   └─ You can do both well (not 50% of each)  
     
   Pros:  
   ✅ Clear consolidation value prop  
   ✅ "One vendor" appeal  
   ✅ Cross-sell between use cases  
     
   Cons:  
   ❌ "Jack of all trades, master of none" risk  
   ❌ Compete with specialists in both categories  
   ❌ Complex positioning  
     
   Examples:  
   ├─ Saviynt: "IGA \+ PAM \+ CSPM" (converged platform)  
   ├─ HubSpot: "CRM \+ Marketing Automation"  
   ├─ Atlassian: "Issue tracking \+ Wiki \+ Chat"  
   └─ IRIS (alternative positioning): "IGA \+ SMP"  
     
   Key Messages:  
   ├─ "Stop managing \[tool 1\] and \[tool 2\] separately"  
   ├─ "One platform for \[use case A\] and \[use case B\]"  
   ├─ "Save \[X%\] vs buying two tools"  
   └─ "Data flows seamlessly between \[A\] and \[B\]"

5\. THE SPECIALIST  
   ──────────────  
   Strategy: Vertical or use-case specialization  
     
   Template: "\[Category\] for \[vertical/use case\]"  
   Example: "IGA for Healthcare" or "IGA for M\&A"  
     
   When to use:  
   ├─ Horizontal category too crowded  
   ├─ Vertical has unique requirements/regulations  
   └─ You have deep vertical expertise  
     
   Pros:  
   ✅ Less competition in niche  
   ✅ Premium pricing (specialization)  
   ✅ Faster sales (credibility in vertical)  
   ✅ Strong word-of-mouth in vertical  
     
   Cons:  
   ❌ Limited TAM  
   ❌ Dependency on vertical health  
   ❌ Harder to expand horizontally later  
     
   Examples:  
   ├─ Veeva: "CRM for Life Sciences"  
   ├─ Toast: "POS for Restaurants"  
   ├─ Procore: "Project Management for Construction"  
   └─ IRIS (alternative): "IGA for Healthcare HIPAA compliance"  
     
   When to specialize:  
   ├─ Vertical \= \>$500M TAM  
   ├─ Unique requirements (not just preferences)  
   ├─ Word-of-mouth strong in vertical  
   └─ Willingness to pay premium for specialization  
     
   Key Messages:  
   ├─ "Built specifically for \[vertical\]"  
   ├─ "We understand \[vertical-specific pain\]"  
   ├─ "Pre-configured for \[vertical regulation\]"  
   └─ "Used by \[X\] of top \[Y\] \[vertical\] companies"

❌ MISTAKE \#1: INSIDE-OUT POSITIONING  
   ────────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We have amazing technology\!"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Leading with technology/features  
   ├─ Talking about HOW product works (architecture, algorithms)  
   └─ Assuming customers care about same things engineers do  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS uses a patented multi-modal discovery engine with   
   9 simultaneous data ingestion pipelines powered by our   
   proprietary graph database."  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Customers don't care about HOW, they care about OUTCOMES  
   ├─ Technical superiority ≠ market success  
   └─ Confuses instead of clarifies  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Start with customer problem, not product features  
   ├─ Lead with business outcomes  
   └─ Mention technology ONLY as proof of capability  
     
   Example (good):  
   "IRIS finds 100% of your SaaS applications—even shadow IT—  
   so you can govern access before it becomes a security risk.   
   Our discovery engine uses 9 methods to ensure nothing slips through."

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#2: BOILING THE OCEAN  
   ───────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We're for everyone\!"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ ICP \= "Any company with 10-10,000 employees"  
   ├─ Value prop lists 15 different use cases  
   └─ Website has 8 different personas  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS is for IT teams, security teams, compliance teams,   
   finance teams, HR teams, and executives who need to manage   
   access, reduce costs, pass audits, improve security,   
   and scale operations."  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Generic messaging doesn't resonate with anyone  
   ├─ Sales doesn't know who to target  
   ├─ Marketing budget spread too thin  
   └─ No word-of-mouth (nobody identifies as "everyone")  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Pick ONE primary target segment  
   ├─ Get specific: Company size, industry, role, pain  
   └─ Dominate one segment, THEN expand  
     
   Example (good):  
   "IRIS is for mid-market IT Directors (500-2,500 employees)   
   managing 100+ SaaS apps who are drowning in manual access   
   requests and dreading their next SOC2 audit."  
     
   Test: "Would this person read that and think 'That's me\!'?"

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#3: COMPETITOR-OBSESSED POSITIONING  
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We're better than \[competitor\]\!"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Website headline: "The \[Competitor\] Alternative"  
   ├─ Every message is comparison vs competitor  
   └─ Positioning defined by what you're NOT  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS is the SailPoint alternative that's faster, cheaper,   
   and easier to use."  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ You're living in competitor's shadow (reinforcing their position)  
   ├─ Only works if everyone knows competitor (many don't)  
   ├─ Limits your positioning (you're "alternative", not "leader")  
   └─ Competitor controls your narrative  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Lead with YOUR value, not competitor's weakness  
   ├─ Define yourself by what you ARE, not what you're NOT  
   └─ Mention competitors in sales battlecards, not positioning  
     
   Example (good):  
   "IRIS is Operational IGA for mid-market IT teams—deploy in   
   60 days and govern 300+ SaaS apps without enterprise complexity."  
     
   (No mention of SailPoint, but differentiation is clear)

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#4: FEATURE PARITY RACE  
   ─────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We have everything they have, plus X\!"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Product roadmap driven by competitor feature list  
   ├─ Messaging focuses on "We have X, Y, Z too\!"  
   └─ Trying to match competitor on every dimension  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS has access certifications ✓, role management ✓,   
   SoD policies ✓, PLUS 300+ SaaS connectors\!"  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ You're playing competitor's game (they define battlefield)  
   ├─ Customers assume incumbent is better at their own game  
   ├─ Feature parity → commodity → price competition  
   └─ Dilutes YOUR unique strengths  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Define NEW dimension where you win  
   ├─ Acknowledge table stakes ("Yes, we have X")  
   ├─ Emphasize unique value  
   └─ Reframe evaluation criteria  
     
   Example (good):  
   "Traditional IGA focuses on audit compliance. IRIS focuses   
   on IT productivity—automate daily operations, get audit-ready   
   as byproduct. Yes, we have certifications and SoD, but that's   
   not what makes us different."

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#5: JARGON OVERLOAD  
   ──────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "Leveraging AI/ML for synergistic value creation"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Buzzword bingo (AI-powered, next-gen, revolutionary)  
   ├─ Industry jargon without explanation  
   └─ Abstract language instead of concrete examples  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS leverages AI/ML-powered predictive analytics to deliver   
   synergistic identity governance across your enterprise ecosystem,   
   enabling zero-trust architecture and digital transformation."  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Nobody talks like this (sounds like marketing BS)  
   ├─ Unclear what product actually does  
   ├─ Triggers skepticism ("What are they hiding?")  
   └─ Doesn't pass "grandma test"  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Use customer language (from interviews)  
   ├─ Be concrete, not abstract  
   ├─ Replace jargon with plain English  
   └─ Pass grandma test (can non-expert understand?)  
     
   Example (good):  
   "IRIS automatically reviews who has access to what across   
   your 300 SaaS apps. When someone joins, moves, or leaves,   
   IRIS handles their access in 3 hours instead of 3 days."  
     
   Test: Read it out loud. Does it sound like how humans talk?

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#6: CATEGORY CONFUSION  
   ─────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We're \[Category A\] and \[Category B\] and \[Category C\]"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Different pages say different categories  
   ├─ Trying to be in multiple categories simultaneously  
   └─ Sales team can't explain "shelf" you're on  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS is an Identity Governance platform, SaaS Management tool,   
   and Cloud Security solution."  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Confusion \= no purchase  
   ├─ Doesn't show up in category searches  
   ├─ Analysts don't know where to rank you  
   └─ "What ARE you?" question from prospects  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Pick ONE primary category  
   ├─ Adjacent categories \= "also does" (not positioning)  
   └─ Be crystal clear about "shelf"  
     
   Example (good):  
   PRIMARY: "IRIS is an Operational IGA platform"  
   ADJACENT: "that includes SaaS discovery capabilities typically   
   found in SaaS Management tools, giving you unified governance."

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#7: ASPIRATIONAL POSITIONING  
   ──────────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We want to be the Nike of \[category\]"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Positioning based on desired brand, not current reality  
   ├─ Claiming market leadership prematurely  
   └─ "Best" or "Leading" without proof  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS is the world's leading IGA platform"  
   (When you have 250 customers vs SailPoint's 2,000)  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Incredibility (prospect thinks "Yeah, right")  
   ├─ Alienates early adopters ("I want underdog, not big vendor")  
   └─ Can't deliver on promise  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Position where you ARE today, not where you want to be  
   ├─ "Leading" must be provable (Gartner Leader, \#1 G2)  
   ├─ Use challenger positioning if not leader  
   └─ Earn leadership over time  
     
   Example (good):  
   "IRIS is the fastest-growing Operational IGA platform,   
   with 300%+ YoY customer growth and 95% retention"  
   (Factual, provable, compelling without false claims)

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#8: IGNORING ALTERNATIVES  
   ─────────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We have no competitors\!"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Positioning doesn't acknowledge alternatives  
   ├─ Assuming customers start from zero  
   └─ No comparison or context  
     
   Example (bad):  
   "IRIS is the IGA platform" (vs what? why not do nothing?)  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Customers ALWAYS have alternatives (even "do nothing")  
   ├─ No context \= no decision (what's the jump they're making?)  
   └─ Sales can't handle "We already use \[X\]" objection  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Explicitly acknowledge alternatives in positioning  
   ├─ Frame value relative to alternatives  
   └─ Help customer understand "jump"  
     
   Example (good):  
   "Mid-market IT teams are stuck between manual spreadsheets   
   (doesn't scale) and SailPoint (18-month implementation).   
   IRIS is the middle ground: enterprise features, 60-day deployment."

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#9: STATIC POSITIONING  
   ─────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "We nailed positioning in 2020, haven't touched it since"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ Positioning unchanged despite market shifts  
   ├─ Ignoring new competitors  
   └─ "If it ain't broke..." mentality  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Markets evolve (new competitors, tech shifts, buyer changes)  
   ├─ Your product evolves (new features, new ICPs)  
   ├─ Customer language evolves  
   └─ Stale positioning \= losing relevance  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ Review positioning quarterly  
   ├─ Incorporate win/loss insights  
   ├─ Track: Is this still resonating? (sales cycle, win rate, conversion)  
   └─ Update when market shifts (new competitor, new trend)  
     
   Triggers to revisit positioning:  
   ├─ Win rate declining \>20%  
   ├─ Sales cycle lengthening \>30%  
   ├─ New competitor changes landscape  
   ├─ Product pivot or major feature launch  
   └─ Customer feedback: "We thought you were \[X\], but you're actually \[Y\]"

   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❌ MISTAKE \#10: DEMOCRATIC POSITIONING  
   ───────────────────────────────────────  
   Symptom: "Let's get everyone's input\!"  
     
   What it looks like:  
   ├─ 20-person positioning committee  
   ├─ Every stakeholder has veto power  
   └─ Positioning by consensus \= bland  
     
   Why it fails:  
   ├─ Too many cooks \= generic positioning  
   ├─ Lowest common denominator (nobody offended, nobody excited)  
   ├─ Months of meetings, no decision  
   └─ Designed to NOT alienate anyone \= resonates with no one  
     
   How to fix:  
   ├─ CEO/CMO owns final decision (not committee)  
   ├─ Gather input, but don't position by consensus  
   ├─ Test with customers, not internal stakeholders  
   └─ Make bold choice (can't please everyone)  
     
   Process:  
   ├─ Workshop: Cross-functional input (1 day)  
   ├─ PMM: Draft positioning based on customer data  
   ├─ CEO/CMO: Review and approve  
   ├─ Validate: Test with customers and sales  
   └─ Commit: Don't iterate based on internal opinions

CONCEPT 1: POSITIONING STACK  
─────────────────────────────────  
Positioning exists at multiple levels simultaneously:

Level 1: CATEGORY (Broadest)  
    "What business are we in?"  
    Example: "We're in the IGA business"  
      
Level 2: POSITION WITHIN CATEGORY  
    "Where do we sit relative to competitors?"  
    Example: "We're the fast/simple IGA vs complex legacy"  
      
Level 3: VALUE PROPOSITION  
    "Why should target customer choose us?"  
    Example: "Deploy in 60 days, automate 85% workflows"  
      
Level 4: MESSAGING  
    "How do we articulate value to different personas?"  
    Example: IT="productivity", CISO="risk", CFO="ROI"  
      
Level 5: FEATURES  
    "What capabilities deliver the value?"  
    Example: "300+ connectors, 9 discovery methods, AI scoring"

All levels must align:  
├─ Features support messaging  
├─ Messaging delivers value prop  
├─ Value prop reinforces position  
├─ Position fits category  
└─ Category matches buyer mental model

Misalignment \= confusion

CONCEPT 3: LADDERING UP (Benefit Chain)  
─────────────────────────────────────────  
Each feature ladders up to higher-order benefit:

Feature (What it is)  
    ↓  
Functional Benefit (What it does)  
    ↓  
Emotional Benefit (How it feels)  
    ↓  
Self-Expressive Benefit (Who I become)

Example: IRIS "300+ SaaS connectors"

Feature:  
"300+ pre-built SaaS connectors"

Functional Benefit:  
"Govern 100% of SaaS stack without custom code"

Emotional Benefit:  
"Sleep soundly knowing no apps slip through the cracks"

Self-Expressive:  
"I'm a proactive IT leader who has control"

Different personas ladder differently:  
├─ IT Director: Function → "Complete coverage"  
├─ CISO: Emotional → "Peace of mind"  
└─ Board: Self-expressive → "Strategic leader"

Position at the level that resonates with decision-maker.

CONCEPT 4: JOBS-TO-BE-DONE (JTBD) POSITIONING  
────────────────────────────────────────────────  
Position around the JOB, not the product:

Traditional: "We are \[category\] that does \[function\]"  
JTBD: "We help \[target\] make progress toward \[goal\]"

Example:  
Traditional: "IRIS is IGA software that manages access"  
JTBD: "IRIS helps IT teams scale operations without scaling headcount"

Shift from WHAT to WHY:  
├─ WHAT: "Access certifications" (feature)  
├─ WHY: "Pass audits without 200-hour scrambles" (job outcome)

Position \= Job \+ Unique approach to job

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONCEPT 5: RESONANCE VS DIFFERENTIATION TRADE-OFF  
───────────────────────────────────────────────────  
Positioning tension:

Resonance: "Customers immediately get it" (familiar category)  
    vs  
Differentiation: "We're uniquely valuable" (novel positioning)

Too much resonance → Commodity ("Just another IGA tool")  
Too much differentiation → Confusion ("What IS this?")

Sweet spot: Familiar \+ Twist

Examples:  
├─ Superhuman: "Email" (familiar) \+ "at speed of thought" (twist)  
├─ Notion: "Workspace" (familiar) \+ "all-in-one" (twist)  
└─ IRIS: "IGA" (familiar) \+ "operational, not compliance" (twist)

80% familiar (category recognition)  
20% different (unique value)

CONCEPT 6: ANCHORING & FRAMING  
────────────────────────────────  
How you FRAME positioning changes perception:

Anchor 1: Competitor  
"Unlike SailPoint's 18-month implementations..."  
→ Frames you as faster alternative

Anchor 2: Status Quo  
"Manual access reviews take 200 hours per quarter..."  
→ Frames you as automation solution

Anchor 3: Aspiration  
"IT teams should be strategic, not firefighting..."  
→ Frames you as enabler of transformation

Choose anchor based on what moves prospect most:  
├─ Frustrated with competitor → Competitor anchor  
├─ Pain of current process → Status quo anchor  
└─ Wants to elevate role → Aspiration anchor

CONCEPT 7: POSITIONING SEQUENCING  
───────────────────────────────────  
When to reveal positioning information:

Homepage (5 seconds):  
├─ Category \+ Primary differentiator  
└─ "Operational IGA for mid-market IT teams"

Demo call (5 minutes):  
├─ Category \+ Value prop \+ Proof  
└─ "IGA for IT productivity, 60-day deployment, 300+ SaaS apps"

Sales presentation (30 minutes):  
├─ Full positioning story (problem → solution → alternatives →   
    differentiation → value → proof)  
└─ Deep dive on "why us"

Case study (written):  
├─ Complete positioning \+ customer validation  
└─ All components with evidence

Don't dump everything at once—sequence based on buyer journey stage.

CONCEPT 8: GEOGRAPHIC & CULTURAL POSITIONING  
──────────────────────────────────────────────  
Positioning varies by market:

US Market:  
├─ Values: Innovation, speed, disruption  
├─ Messaging: "Revolutionary," "Next-gen," "Game-changer"  
└─ Risk tolerance: Higher (willing to try new category)

European Market:  
├─ Values: Stability, compliance, reliability  
├─ Messaging: "Proven," "Compliant," "Trusted"  
└─ Risk tolerance: Lower (prefer established categories)

APAC Market:  
├─ Values: Relationship, harmony, collective benefit  
├─ Messaging: "Partnership," "Long-term," "Community"  
└─ Risk tolerance: Varies by country

Adjust positioning while keeping core differentiation.

CONCEPT 9: POSITIONING EVOLUTION  
──────────────────────────────────  
Positioning changes over company lifecycle:

Early Stage (Seed/Series A):  
├─ Niche positioning (specific ICP)  
├─ "IGA for healthcare HIPAA compliance"  
└─ Validate product-market fit

Growth Stage (Series B/C):  
├─ Broader positioning (expand ICP)  
├─ "IGA for regulated industries"  
└─ Scale go-to-market

Maturity (Series D+/Public):  
├─ Category positioning (own category)  
├─ "The IGA leader for enterprises"  
└─ Defend market share

Don't position like mature company when you're early stage.

CONCEPT 10: POSITIONING AS STRATEGY FILTER  
─────────────────────────────────────────────  
Positioning guides ALL decisions:

Product:  
├─ Build features that reinforce positioning  
├─ "Operational IGA" → Prioritize IT productivity features  
└─ Reject features that dilute positioning

Marketing:  
├─ Content themes align with positioning  
├─ "60-day deployment" → Case studies showing speed  
└─ Events targeting IT Directors, not GRC/compliance conferences

Sales:  
├─ Target accounts matching ICP  
├─ "Mid-market" → Focus on 500-5K employee companies  
└─ Disqualify enterprises wanting "SailPoint-like" complexity

Partnerships:  
├─ Partner with companies that reinforce positioning  
├─ "SaaS-first" → Integrate with Salesforce, Workday, not mainframes  
└─ Co-market with complementary (not competitive) tools

Every decision asks: "Does this strengthen or weaken our positioning?"
